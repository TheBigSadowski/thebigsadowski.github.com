Segment and route discovery

I put some of my ideas around this into the chrome plugin I talk about above. It adds a small section to the right side navigation on segment pages that lets you jump to other segments that are nearby. It also changes how the "Explore" tab works, to take you to a segment explore page that is focused on the area around the segment you were looking at.

Ideally, this could also be something more like "people that rode this segment also rode these segments" or with stared segments, we could find people with similar tastes and use that information to suggest new routes for people.

Percentile data

Since I'm not the world's fastest (yet!), it would be interesting to see what percentile I am in as compared to other riders and be able to trend this over time. This comparison could be on a segment by segment level. It could just be a note next to each segment that you ride, telling you what percentile you are in. For example, If I had the 1208th fastest time out of 6023 riders on a segment, I would be in the 79% percentile.

You could take this even further and give each rider an overall percentile based on all the segments they have ridden. This could really encourage people to get out there and strive for personal improvement.

Reopen the API

I know your community is asking for this. I just think it would be awesome to work on. I'd love to see what people can do with the tools and data you could provide.

Why you might want me to work with you

To save you from having to read through all of my past experience on my resume, here are a few highlights that I think you will find most relevant to what you are doing at Strava...

I've scaled large apps before

While working on the Business Catalyst team (before and after Adobe acquired us), I reworked, tuned and rebuilt (when needed) the more performance critical parts of the system to allow us to scale from a few thousand hosted websites up to over 30 thousand paying websites running on our platform. This comprised of profiling application, database and cache level components and tuning them to be sometimes 400x more performant.

One of the best examples of this was when our blogging engine hit a wall. At the time, Jessica Watson was using our platform to write about her experiences while setting the world record for the youngest person to sail around the world alone. This was heavily publicized in the Australia media and and drove massive (and unexpected) amounts of traffic to the blogging engine. Enough to take our services offline in Australia at times. To fix this, I went to work profiling the code, reworking the database schemas and indexes and moving as much of the content to pre-rendered chunks and storing them in memcache as possible. This resulted in us being able to handle over 400 times the traffic to our blogging engine and I'm proud to say there has been no need for future performance tuning on Blogs.

I've built, run and maintained location based systems that deal specifically with neighborhoods

At CityVoter, we ran best of contests to find the best local small businesses in cities and neighborhoods. Because of this, I've either personally developed or led the development of things that are probably very similar to parts of what you've built. For example, we needed to be able to define city and neighborhood boundaries and check to make sure businesses were within the boundaries. We solved this by building admin tools to draw the neighborhoods and other geographic boundaries and code to match the businesses location to that geography. We also needed to verify business ownership and chose to use a combination of ways similar to what you have done.

I've built two extremely high performing agile development groups

We used a kanban board to keep track of what stage the development of each feature was in and to be able to focus attention when things became stuck.
