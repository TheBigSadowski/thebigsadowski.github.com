Why you might want me to work with you

To save you from having to read through all of my past experience on my resume, here are a few highlights that I think you will find most relevant to what you are doing at Lookout...

I've lead, mentored and managed extremely high-performing lean/agile teams

At Rakuten, not only was I able to set up processes and team structure in a way that increased team output by about 4x what it was perviously, but I was able to also teach people the benefits of many lean/agile techniques, leading to teams that self-improved constantly and really embraced concepts like automated testing and continuous deployment. The coolest part about this is that some of these people have moved to other teams and introduced the practices they learned to improve the way those teams work.

Security has always been a huge concern of mine

While developing ad servers for Rakuten, I asked the team to take a week off from the normal development cycle and to focus on security as well as performance. We did this by asking the team to break into two groups. One would attack the system, trying to overload or otherwise break it, and the other would try to quickly react to the attacks and patch the system to foil the attack. If the attackers could take down the service for more than 5 minutes a day (as monitored by pingdom) they would win the day, if the service had less than 5 minutes of downtime, the defenders would win. The outcome was that the teams had a blast fighting it out, learned new techniques for both attacking and defending our services and we were able to harden the system from attacks and prove the amount of traffic we can handle at the same time.

If you're curious about some of the details, one of the developers wrote up how he was able to implement some of the defenses on his blog.

I've built two extremely high performing agile development groups

At both CityVoter and Rakuten, I've led and mentored teams in how to design a system and process to be fully confident in being able to deploy many times per day. At Rakuten, we were able to deliver new API level features to the team in Tokyo that was building the Japanese language version of the user interface and integrate their code back in every day. We used a kanban board to keep track of what stage the development of each feature was in and to be able to focus attention when things became stuck. All of this led to much faster delivery of features and an end to end knowledge of where we were at with the project that we didn't have before.

And at CityVoter, the team I built form the ground up still carries my personal best for number of successful no-outage releases to production in a single day... 26 times.
